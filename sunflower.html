Sunflower

Back to Grow

  1. Choosing the Right Variety

    Start by selecting the right type of sunflower for your garden. Common varieties for beginners include the Dwarf Sunflower or the Giant Sunflower. Consider your available space and the desired height of your sunflowers when making your choice.

  2. Selecting a Sunny Spot

    Sunflowers thrive in full sunlight, so choose a location in your garden that receives at least 6-8 hours of direct sunlight each day. Ensure the soil in this area is well-drained to prevent waterlogging.

  3. Planting Sunflower Seeds

    Plant sunflower seeds directly in the ground after the last frost date in your region. Dig a small hole about 1 inch deep and place one seed in each hole. Space the holes at least 6-12 inches apart, depending on the variety. Water the seeds well after planting.

  4. Watering and Care

    Sunflowers are relatively low-maintenance, but they do require regular watering. Keep the soil consistently moist, especially during dry spells. Once the sunflowers reach a height of around 12 inches, you can reduce the frequency of watering. Fertilize sparingly with a balanced, all-purpose fertilizer.

  5. Supporting Tall Sunflowers

    If you're growing taller sunflower varieties, consider providing support to prevent them from bending or falling over. You can use stakes or garden twine to gently tie the stems to keep them upright. Be mindful of any strong winds in your area.
